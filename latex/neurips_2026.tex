\begin{abstract}
  This project is about structured information extraction from clinical abstracts using the EBM-NLP dataset, with the goal of categorising Population, Intervention, Comparator, and Outcome (PICO) elements from unstructured text. The core challenge lies in the lexical variability of biomedical language, where target information is frequently expressed indirectly, distributed across sentences, or annotated inconsistently across fields. A preliminary clustering analysis confirmed that PICO entity types do not naturally separate into clusters in the embedding space, motivating the use of more strategic extraction approaches. We designed two axes: rule-based versus LLM-based extraction, and end-to-end versus decomposed pipelines. LLM-based methods using Claude Sonnet substantially outperformed the rule-based baseline, with overall token F1 improving from 0.21 to 0.47. The decomposed pipeline achieved strong Intervention extraction (F1 = 0.35) but did not succeed on Population and Comparator in the few-shot setting. Across both axes, Outcome extraction remained the most difficult field, with coverage below 50\% for all methods. These suggest that prompt-based LLM extraction is the most practical approach in low-resource settings, but further gains and more improvements would likely require fine-tuning or richer supervision.
\end{abstract}



\section{Introduction}
The abstracts of clinical trials contain important information about study design, participant groups, treatments, and results. However, this information is presented in unstructured text, making it difficult to extract and compare efficiently across studies. Converting such abstracts into structured tables can support faster evidence synthesis, make relevant information easier to locate and analyse, and help researchers and clinicians compare findings across a large number of studies. This creates a useful setting for studying how different information extraction strategies perform on biomedical text.


\subsection{Task Overview}


This project addresses the task of \textbf{structured information extraction} from clinical trial abstracts in the \textbf{EBM-NLP} [1] dataset. The objective is to identify and extract \textbf{Population, Intervention, Comparator, and Outcome (PICO)} information from unstructured abstracts and represent it in a schema. The task also includes \textbf{preliminary clustering analysis} to examine whether natural clusters correspond to schema fields, as well as comparison of alternative extraction pipelines across \textbf{two design axes}. We then evaluate these approaches in terms of \textbf{field-level extraction quality}, \textbf{coverage versus precision}, and \textbf{downstream usability}, and discuss the trade-offs and limitations of the task.

\subsection{Motivation and Challenges}


An extraction of structured information from clinical trial abstracts is valuable because important study details are frequently buried in free-text descriptions. Automatically identifying key fields such as Population, Intervention, Comparator, and Outcome can make trial evidence easier to manage and analyse at scale. However, this task is challenging because clinical abstracts are written in varied and compact language, and relevant information is probably distributed across various sentences rather than stated in a single standard form. Moreover, some fields can overlap semantically or be difficult to distinguish, particularly when interventions and comparators are described in similar ways. 

A further practical challenge is that comparator information is not represented as clearly as the other target fields in the dataset setup used in our experiments, which makes full PICO evaluation more difficult. 

A useful extraction system should not only require good field-level performance, but also balance between coverage and precision, while generating outputs that remain usable for downstream querying and analysis.

\subsection{Dataset and Problem Characteristics}

The \textbf{EBM-NLP} dataset contains clinical trial abstracts paired with annotated entities for structured information extraction. In the overall task setting, we aim to recover key study information from unstructured abstracts and map it into a predefined schema containing \textbf{Population}, \textbf{Intervention}, \textbf{Comparator}, and \textbf{Outcome} elements. However, in the annotation setting used in our experiments, the available labels more clearly support \textbf{Population}, \textbf{Intervention}, and \textbf{Outcome (PIO)} extraction, while comparator information is not explicitly shown. This affects both the scope of our analysis and the difficulty of a full PICO extraction and evaluation.

In our processing pipeline, the original annotations were converted into \textbf{BIO} format in order to represent span boundaries explicitly for extraction and analysis. For the main task setting in our experiments, these annotations were grouped into broader \textbf{Population}, \textbf{Intervention}, and \textbf{Outcome} categories. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:pio_labels}, each field also contains subtype labels. This means that the dataset captures not only the top PIO structure, but also more detailed semantic distinctions within each field.

\subsection{Requirements for a Good Solution}

Hence, a good solution for this task should cover the correct target fields from unstructured clinical trial abstracts and map them into a usable structured format. Only a strong field-level extraction performance alone is not enough. The system should also balance coverage and precision, handle variation in wording and sentence structure across abstracts, and generate outputs that remain useful for downstream querying. For these reasons, we compare multiple extraction axes to perform the best possible information extraction. The details of these strategies are presented in the following section.


\section{Methods}

In this section, we outline the methodological design of our study. Beginning with a preliminary clustering analysis to examine the semantic structure of the annotated fields, and then describing the extraction pipelines used for comparison, including the baseline system and alternative strategy variants to improve our work.
\label{gen_inst}

\subsection{Preliminary Clustering Analysis}

Before moving to the extraction stage, we first carried out a preliminary clustering analysis as an early exploratory step. This aims to examine whether the target schema fields showed any natural semantic grouping in the dataset before we designed the extraction pipelines. To investigate latent structure in the data, we used \textbf{K-Means clustering}, which offers a straightforward partition-based grouping.

In our implementation, we did not cluster full sentences directly, since most single sentences contain multiple annotated entities corresponding to different schema fields. Using the whole sentence with a single embedding can therefore lead to unclear distinctions between entity types that are important for this task. Instead of that approach, we clustered \textbf{annotated entity spans}. This step was intended as a diagnostic step rather than a complete extraction method."

\subsubsection{Clustering with K-Means}

In this part, we used K-Means clustering to check whether the embedded text spans could naturally form groups similar to the three target entity types: Population, Intervention, and Outcome. The clustering data was created by combining the tokenised spans from these three categories into one dataset. In total, the dataset contained 14,036 text spans. Each span was then represented as a dense embedding using the BioLORD-2023 model [2], giving a numerical vector for each text span.

Because the extraction task focuses on three entity types, we set the number of clusters to three. After the clustering process, we used UMAP to reduce the high-dimensional embeddings into two dimensions. This made it possible to visualise the clustering results and compare them with the original Population, Intervention, and Outcome labels, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:kmeans_umap}.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\columnwidth]{k_means_output.png}
\caption{UMAP visualisation of BioLORD-2023 embeddings for 14,036 PIO spans. Left: true P/I/O labels. Right: K-Means cluster assignments (k=3).}
\label{fig:kmeans_umap}
\end{figure}

The visualisation of the true labels shows that the three entity types are heavily mixed together because most points appear in the same general locations rather than forming three clearly separated areas. This suggests that the semantic difference between the three categories is not strong enough to be captured by simple unsupervised clustering. In clinical abstracts, this is understandable because P, I, and O phrases often share similar medical vocabulary and context. For example, patient groups, treatments, and outcomes may all contain related disease names, measurements, or clinical terms.

Furthermore, the K-Means result shows a geometric division of the embedding space, but this division does not match the true entity labels well enough. Because of the evaluation scores, K-Means achieved an Adjusted Rand Index of 0.1629 and a Normalized Mutual Information score of 0.1929, with a Silhouette Score of 0.0409. While these values indicate a weak but non-trivial agreement with the true labels, the overall alignment remains poor, and the low Silhouette Score also confirms that the clusters are not well-separated in the embedding space.


\subsection{Baseline Extraction System}

Our baseline system is a \textbf{rule-based extraction pipeline} designed to recover \textbf{Population}, \textbf{Intervention}, \textbf{Comparator}, and \textbf{Outcome} information from clinical trial abstracts. The system will combine \textbf{regular expressions}, \textbf{part-of-speech (POS) tag patterns}, and \textbf{domain-specific keyword lists} to identify candidate spans for each target field. No external machine learning models are used since the method relies entirely on interpretable linguistic patterns derived from the structure of biomedical text.

\subsubsection{Preprocessing}

Each abstract is loaded from the EBM-NLP dataset as a plain text file (\texttt{.txt}). Two additional files are also loaded for each document: a tokenised word list (\texttt{.tokens}) and a corresponding sequence of POS tags (\texttt{.pos}), both provided by the dataset. These inputs are used directly by the rule-based pipeline during extraction.

The hierarchical annotation files (\texttt{.AGGREGATED.ann}) are \textbf{not} used as inputs to the extraction process itself. Instead, they are used only to obtain \textbf{ground truth spans} for evaluation. In our experimental setting, ground truth spans for \textbf{Population}, \textbf{Intervention}, and \textbf{Outcome} are derived from these annotations, while \textbf{Comparator} ground truth is approximated using \textbf{Intervention label 7}, as mentioned in Section 1.3, which corresponds to control-group spans.

\subsubsection{Extraction Pipeline}

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{baseline.drawio.pdf}
    \caption{Overview of the rule-based baseline extraction pipeline. The system uses raw abstract text together with token and POS information, then applies a three-step cascade of regex matching, POS-based fallback, and keyword fallback to produce structured output fields for evaluation.}
    \label{fig:baseline_pipeline}
\end{figure}

For each target field, the baseline system applies a \textbf{three-step priority cascade}. The extraction process starts with the most specific rules and then falls back to more general heuristics if no match is found.

\textbf{Step 1: Regex patterns.}
A set of regular expressions is applied to the raw abstract text in order of specificity, and the first successful match is returned. For example, \textit{Population} extraction first attempts to identify expressions containing a cardinal number followed by a participant-related noun, such as ``200 patients'', before falling back to broader patterns that capture patient-related phrases with nearby context.

\textbf{Step 2: POS-based fallback.}
If no regex pattern matches, the system scans the POS tag sequence for field-specific syntactic patterns. For \textit{Population}, this includes patterns such as a cardinal number tag (\texttt{CD}) followed by a singular or plural noun (\texttt{NN}/\texttt{NNS}). For \textit{Intervention} and \textit{Outcome}, the system identifies trigger verbs and collects the following noun-phrase-like tokens as candidate spans.

\textbf{Step 3: Keyword fallback.}
If both previous steps fail, the system returns a keyword match from a predefined domain vocabulary. For example, \textit{Outcome} extraction falls back to terms such as ``mortality'', ``pain'', or ``blood pressure''.

Table~\ref{tab:baseline_rules} provides representative examples of the main rule types used for each field, rather than an exhaustive list of all implemented patterns. These examples are intended to illustrate the overall rule design. In practice, each field is handled by a broader set of regex patterns, POS-based heuristics, and fallback keywords.

\subsubsection{Limitations of the Baseline}

The rule-based system outputs only a single span per field, which limits recall when multiple spans are present in the ground truth annotations. In addition, the method is sensitive to wording variation and cannot reliably resolve lexical ambiguity. For example, the conjunction ``or'' may indicate a true comparator relation, as in ``drug A or placebo'', but it may also simply connect related expressions, such as ``efficacy or safety'', leading to false positives in comparator extraction. More generally, the system depends on handcrafted lexical and syntactic patterns, it may struggle when important information is expressed indirectly, distributed across sentences, or described using wording that not covered by the predefined rules.

The second axis focuses on \textbf{pipeline design}, comparing \textbf{end-to-end} extraction with a more \textbf{decomposed} substep pipeline. \paragraph{Hypotheses and Proposed Improvement.}\mbox{}\\
We hypothesise that when field information is expressed using predictable lexical patterns in clearly structured abstracts, the \textbf{rule-based baseline} may have high precision. It is anticipated that the \textbf{few-shot LLM} will better than zero-shot by offering examples that enable the model to more closely adhere to the necessary output structure, reduce hallucinations across fields, and more consistently distinguish Comparator from Intervention.


\subsubsection{Axis 2: End-to-end vs Decomposed Extraction}
The second experimental axis compares \textbf{end-to-end} and \textbf{decomposed extraction} pipelines. This comparison was designed to examine whether structured information extraction is better handled as a single direct prediction problem or as a sequence of smaller, more focused subtasks. We first implemented the end-to-end pipeline as a direct baseline, where the model predicts token-level labels in one stage under zero-shot, few-shot, and full-train settings. We then implemented the decomposed pipeline and evaluated it under the same settings, allowing us to compare both formulations consistently across the three schema fields and to interpret their extraction behaviour in relation to one another.

\paragraph{Hypotheses and Proposed Improvement.}\mbox{}\\
We hypothesise that the \textbf{end-to-end pipeline} can perform well when relevant information is expressed clearly and the model is able to learn stable token-level patterns directly from the data. However, its performance may be limited when the task requires handling multiple forms of variation simultaneously. The \textbf{decomposed pipeline} is expected to perform better when some supervision is available, as each stage addresses a narrower subproblem and can make more effective use of the training signal. However, decomposition may introduce error propagation if relevant content is missed in an earlier stage. For this reason, we expected little difference between the two approaches in the zero-shot setting, but anticipated the decomposed method to become more advantageous in the few-shot and full-train settings.

\section{Experimental evaluation}
\label{headings}

\subsection{Axis 1: Rule-based vs LLM-based Extraction}

\subsubsection{Performance Metrics}

We evaluate all extraction methods using \textbf{token-level partial match F1}, measuring token overlap between predicted and gold-annotated spans. Precision is the fraction of predicted tokens appearing in the gold set, recall is the fraction of gold tokens covered by the prediction, and F1 is their harmonic mean. This metric was chosen because exact string matching would penalise semantically correct extractions scores that differ only in boundary placement. We additionally report \textbf{span-level coverage} and \textbf{downstream usability}, measured by the frequency of field-relevant keywords in the extracted outputs.

\subsubsection{Experimental Setup}

All methods were evaluated on the 184 test documents common to all three EBM-NLP entity types (Population, Intervention, Outcome), with Comparator ground truth approximated using Intervention label 7 as described in Section 2.2. No training data was used for the rule-based baseline or the LLM-based methods; both Claude zero-shot and few-shot variants were evaluated in a fully held-out setting without fine-tuning.

For the LLM-based approaches, we used \texttt{claude-sonnet-4-6} via the Anthropic API\footnote{\url{https://www.anthropic.com/api}}. The zero-shot prompt instructed the model to extract PICO elements and return results in JSON format, with each value required to be a phrase copied directly from the abstract.\footnote{System instruction: ``You are a medical information extraction system. Extract the PICO elements from the following clinical trial abstract. Return the result in JSON format with keys: Population, Intervention, Comparator, Outcome. Each value must be a phrase copied EXACTLY from the abstract. Use null if not found.''} The few-shot prompt extended this with five manually selected demonstration examples drawn from training documents. Notably, the demonstrations used a short \texttt{P/I/C/O} label format rather than JSON, introducing a format mismatch between the examples and the requested output structure, which may partly explain the modest gain of few-shot over zero-shot. The rule-based baseline was applied directly to the tokenised test documents using the cascade described in Section 2.2.2.

\subsubsection{Error Analysis}

A concrete illustration of rule-based failure modes is provided by a document comparing budesonide Turbuhaler with budesonide aqua for seasonal allergic rhinitis. The rule-based system extracts ``400 microg/day'' as the Population span, a consequence of its cardinal-number-plus-noun POS pattern matching a dosage figure rather than a participant description. For Intervention, the system returns an over-extended span conflating both treatment arms, and Comparator extraction returns null entirely as no comparative marker pattern fires. By contrast, both Claude zero-shot and few-shot correctly identify the patient population, intervention, and comparator spans.

For the LLM-based methods, the dominant error is boundary over-extension in Outcome extraction, where the zero-shot model returns a full result sentence while the few-shot model returns a more focused noun phrase. A further error pattern is field confusion in the rule-based system, where ``placebo'' is frequently misassigned to the Intervention field rather than Comparator, a problem largely absent in both LLM-based methods. Together, these error patterns suggest that while LLM-based methods handle lexical variability more robustly, precise boundary control and schema consistency remain challenging across all approaches.


\subsection{Axis 2: End-to-end vs Decomposed Extraction}

\subsubsection{Performance Metrics}

We evaluate Axis 2 using the final extraction scores reported by the end-to-end and decomposed notebooks. For the end-to-end pipeline, predicted BIO tags are converted into text spans and compared with gold spans using exact span matching after lowercasing and whitespace normalisation, with precision, recall, and F1 computed in the standard way. For the decomposed pipeline, we report pipeline precision, recall, and F1 after sentence filtering and token extraction, reflecting the performance of the full system rather than the oracle token stage. The decomposed method also includes a dedicated Comparator evaluation based on the gold \texttt{CONTROL} subtype within the intervention labels, allowing Comparator to be reported alongside Population, Intervention, and Outcome. Sentence-level results and span-level coverage are additionally reported to help identify whether the main bottleneck lies in sentence filtering or token extraction, with coverage defined as recall expressed as a percentage.

\subsubsection{Experimental Setup}

Both methods use the aggregated hierarchical labels from EBM-NLP, but differ substantially in formulation. The end-to-end notebook collapses these labels into coarse BIO tags and trains a logistic regression token classifier with handcrafted lexical and local-context features, reporting zero-shot, few-shot, and full-train settings; few-shot uses 100 training documents per field, while full-train uses the complete training split. The decomposed method instead applies a two-stage Bio ClinicalBERT pipeline, first filtering sentences likely to contain Population, Intervention, or Outcome information, and then performing fine-grained token extraction on the retained sentences. Its saved outputs report zero-shot and few-shot settings, with few-shot using 40 training documents per field. Unlike the end-to-end method, it explicitly distinguishes typed labels such as \texttt{B-AGE}, \texttt{B-DRUG}, and \texttt{B-CONTROL}, allowing Comparator to be evaluated directly from the predicted \texttt{CONTROL} subtype.

\subsubsection{Error Analysis}

A clear contrast between the two pipelines appears in Intervention extraction. The decomposed few-shot system reaches F1 = 0.35, well above the end-to-end few-shot score of 0.15 and the end-to-end full-train score of 0.12. This suggests that filtering intervention-relevant sentences before token extraction is beneficial when the field is lexically distinctive and clinically well localised.

However, the same decomposed pipeline fails completely on Population at the token stage, despite strong sentence filtering. This indicates that the main bottleneck is not sentence routing alone, but the downstream token extractor’s inability to recover reliable participant spans.

The end-to-end pipeline shows a different failure mode. Few-shot improves clearly over zero-shot, while full-train mainly increases recall rather than balanced extraction quality. Although full-train recovers more gold spans across Population, Intervention, and Outcome, precision drops enough that F1 does not exceed the few-shot setting. Overall, the decomposed design has greater potential when the sentence and token stages align well, whereas the end-to-end approach is more robust but less capable of strong field-specific gains.


\subsection{Discussion}

The results across both axes reveal a consistent pattern: approaches with greater semantic understanding achieve better coverage, but this does not always translate into higher precision or schema consistency.
In Axis 1, LLM-based methods substantially outperform the rule-based baseline, with overall F1 more than doubling from 0.21 to 0.47. However, the modest gain of few-shot over zero-shot suggests that a small number of demonstrations is insufficient to resolve deeper ambiguities, particularly for Comparator, which shares surface form with Intervention.
In Axis 2, the decomposed pipeline shows higher potential for fields where sentence filtering works well, as seen in Intervention (F1 = 0.35), but fails completely on Population and Comparator in the few-shot setting. The end-to-end approach is more consistent but achieves lower overall performance. Comparing both axes, LLM-based extraction achieves more consistent results across all four PICO fields, suggesting it is a more practical choice in low-resource settings. However, prompt-only approaches have a performance ceiling, and further improvement would likely require fine-tuning.
A shared limitation across all methods is Outcome extraction, which remains below 50\% coverage even for the best approach, reflecting the heterogeneous nature of Outcome annotations in EBM-NLP.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{others}

For Axis 1, the results demonstrate clearly that LLM-based extraction substantially outperforms the rule-based baseline across all PICO fields, with overall token-level F1 more than doubling from 0.21 to approximately 0.46--0.47. The most significant gains were observed for Population and Comparator, where the rule-based system struggled most with lexical variability and boundary detection. The concrete failure modes of the rule-based approach, such as extracting dosage figures as Population spans due to cardinal-number POS patterns, conflating intervention and comparator arms into a single span, and returning null for Comparator when no explicit comparative marker was present, highlight the fundamental brittleness of handcrafted rules when applied to the diverse phrasing of clinical trial abstracts. That said, the rule-based system did achieve moderate precision for Comparator and Population when patterns were predictable, suggesting it may still be useful as a lightweight fallback in constrained settings.

Few-shot prompting offered consistent but modest improvements over zero-shot, most evident for Comparator and Intervention. However, the gains were smaller than anticipated, which we attribute partly to a format mismatch between the \texttt{P/I/C/O} label format used in the demonstration examples and the JSON output structure requested in the prompt. This finding highlights the sensitivity of few-shot LLM performance to the consistency between demonstration format and output format, and suggests that more carefully aligned examples could yield larger improvements.

Despite the clear advantage of LLM-based methods, several challenges remain open. Recall remains structurally limited by the single-span-per-field constraint, as the ground truth frequently contains multiple non-contiguous spans per field. Our exploration of a multi-span extraction variant showed that prompting the model to return lists of spans improved coverage but introduced hallucination, particularly for fields with fewer annotated spans such as Comparator, where the model over-generated additional spans likely influenced by the distribution of the more span-rich fields it had been exposed to during pretraining. Downstream usability analysis further revealed that even the best-performing LLM variant extracted overly specific outcome phrases that do not align with canonical clinical terminology, limiting their utility for aggregation or querying. Addressing these challenges through better output validation, post-processing, or more structured prompting strategies remains an important direction for future work.
